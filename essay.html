COPPA

The internet is a extremely useful tool and resource for information to those of all ages. However, it is so widespread and difficult to monitor that parents are having a hard time protecting one of the most easily influenced age groups, young children. Not only does the internet act as a tool for finding useful information, but it can also be used as a tool for communication between a wide range of online users. This enables users to communicate anonymously making it very difficult for you to really know who you are talking to. It is extremely difficult for parents to monitor their child’s activities on the internet. In order to help parents better protect their children from malicious internet users COPPA was created.

COPPA, or the Child Online Privacy Protection Act, requires that the Federal Trade Commission regulate the usage and collection of the personal information of minors under thirteen. Enacted in 1998 by legislators in hopes of “protecting children from predatory marketing, physical safety risks such as stalking or kidnapping, and other abuses that may result from others’ access to children’s private data.” As parents and legislators were fearful of the how dangerous the internet could be as it grew and it’s new wide scale availability, COPPA was enacted to “provide parents with better tools to protect their children in an online era” (Bryan, 1998).

In 1998 there were almost 10 million active users on the internet below the age of 13 in the United States alone. This is a large amount of minors at risk from unregulated data collection. In a time where the internet was largely unknown and security was minimal and/or completely lacking, the Child Online Privacy Protection Act was in put place. It was designed to help parents corral and control what personal information could be captured and collected by others about their children’s internet use. Information that is collected on websites such as telephone numbers, addresses, names, social security numbers, and photographs are all covered by COPPA’s rules and regulations which classified these details as personal information.

With the internet, people all over the world can anonymously talk to the most susceptible group, children. Monitoring minors is extremely difficult on the internet, especially when it was new and mostly uncharted. Chatrooms and forums popping up online regularly allowed anonymous communication. COPPA makes websites require parent’s permission to allow a minor under thirteen to make an account, but it is difficult to confirm that the “parent” actually gave consent. To combat this, many websites simply ban users under thirteen years of age from their terms of service to bypass these regulations and requirements. Many minors can easily get around this law that is trying to protect them by lying about their age online. They simply enter an older, false date of birth, allowing websites to collect information on them under the guise that they are “adult” members bypassing COPPA’s laws. One effort to help this COPPA does is requiring websites targeting youth to have a mechanism to require parent permission, often through use of a credit card.

Despite COPPA’s purpose to protect children and be a positive factor online, it tends to create frustrations for online companies tailored towards kids. Due to COPPA guidelines, they continually must jump through hoops to have their website approved, and many of these hoops can create frustration for parents and kids as well. These may include things like requiring credit card information, calling toll-free numbers, and accepting digital signatures to verify age and identity. These official procedures, although there to protect, can be frustrating and annoying. Does this actually protect though? Kids can easily work around these issues to get where they want to go. The best solution to this issue is parental controls. There are also now tablets and computers designed for kids, giving the parent control of all content they are able to see, and access they are given for downloading, buying, etc.
